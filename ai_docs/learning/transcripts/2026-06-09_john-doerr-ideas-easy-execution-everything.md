## Format
Doerr opens by mapping the room (engineers / would-be founders) and crowd-sourcing questions onto a
whiteboard grouped into: career & leadership, startups & entrepreneurship, the next big thing, venture
capital, policy & politics, personal, education, healthcare, China, women in tech. He answers a subset,
then gives an overall framework, then takes more questions.

## Next big things / green tech (~7:00–14:00)
- Energy is a ~$6T/yr global system; current path unsustainable (~120M tons CO2/day — "global weirding,"
  not just warming). Admits KP "invested too much, too fast" in green and didn't get dramatic innovations.
  Wins cited: Opower (behavior change), Nest (distributed thermostats), Tesla (vanguard of electrification).
- "Green is not a sector, it's a spectrum" — some parts suit entrepreneurs, some guarantee failure.
- The game-changer he bets on: **radically better batteries**. Claims he held a cell with ~250% the energy
  density of best lithium-ion → ~40% of the cost per unit energy → EVs at internal-combustion cost →
  scalable decarbonization of transport. "We have all the technology we need to deal with this."
- Other excitements: digital health, Magic Leap (AR that respects eye physics), "SoLoMo" (social/local/
  mobile), the smartphone as "your remote controller for the world," security ("two kinds of institutions:
  hacked and know it, hacked and don't").

## Venture capital — the 5 factors (~14:50–17:45)
What distinguishes the Amazons/Googles from the average venture:
1. **Technical excellence** — not for papers/PhDs, but to attract the best technical talent (A-players want
   to work with A-players).
2. **Outstanding founders & management** — deep belief in founder-led companies; smart founders who can't
   scale will get a COO/CEO. 
3. **Strategic focus on a large new / unserved market.**
4. **Execution = speed.** "Innovation without execution is a hallucination" (Edison). Execution at speed is
   everything.
5. **Reasonable financings** — staged. Don't raise too much OR too little ("when you run out of money it's
   over"). The way to build value: be ruthlessly, intellectually honest about your venture's biggest RISK,
   then get that risk out front and removed (can the product work? can you close the key data partner?).

## How he evaluates new fields (~17:45–19:30)
"Hang around the smartest people I can." Builds a network of advisers; worships innovators; ~book a week;
6 print newspapers + ~35 magazines (MIT Tech Review, Economist); asks entrepreneurs "what should I read/
watch"; cold-calls innovators for breakfast. Doesn't need to be the expert — needs taste, and relies on
entrepreneurs as the thought leaders.

## Qualities of entrepreneurs (~19:38–21:00)
Pattern-matching. The Bezos story — walked into a seedy Seattle office (free needle clinic across the
street), Bezos bounded down with a honking laugh and intense eyes; Doerr knew "I wouldn't mind being in
trouble with Jeff." With world-changing founders "we ARE going to get in trouble" — the goal is to make
NEW mistakes, not repeat old ones.

## Mentoring & coaching (~21:09–23:30)
Recommends every entrepreneur get a coach. The Bill Campbell story: Doerr told Eric Schmidt (new at Google)
to get a coach; Schmidt was offended, then called it the best advice he ever got. A great coach: gets fully
aligned with you, has your back, gives **constructive critical feedback** (the rarest thing in business),
and works with your whole team. "Helps the process go quickly" — doesn't make the decisions.

## Career framework — the heart of the talk (~23:30–31:30)
- **Top priority when choosing what's next: LEARNING & GROWING, not money.** Build a strong foundation of
  experiences.
- If you'll be a founder/CEO, **learn to sell** ("if customers won't pay for what you're doing, you'll
  fail") and **learn to manage** a team of ~a dozen — learn these "on somebody else's nickel."
- **Network relentlessly** — personal, lifelong networks (not LinkedIn). Cold-call anyone who matters.
  Doerr interviewed 35 companies for his first valley job just to build the network. Get good at public
  speaking ("people are judged by their ability to think and speak on their feet more than any other skill").
- **Get in on the ground floor of a company that could be enormous** — great people to learn from, and you
  get pulled up as it grows without elbowing anyone out (Intel in '74, Google in '01).
- **Be choiceful about your FIELD** — easier to change company than field. Aim to be a **leader**: "ideas
  are relatively easy; it's TEAMS that make stuff happen."
- **Missionaries vs Mercenaries** (from Randy Komisar's *The Monk and the Riddle*, funeral.com→community-of-
  the-bereaved pivot story): mercenaries are opportunistic, obsess on competitors, sprint, financial
  statements, entitlement, "deferred life plan." Missionaries are strategic, obsess on customers, run a
  marathon, value statements, mentoring, whole-life balance. "The difference between a lust for making money
  vs a lust for making MEANING and money." Doing significant work → a good life well-led.
- Closing refrain: **"Ideas are easy, execution is everything. It takes a team to win."**

## Education & healthcare (~34:17–40:15)
- US K-12 is "failing — killing our kids" (20% don't learn to read / do algebra). Hope: Khan Academy (10M
  students/mo, "faculty of one," no district/union approval needed) + smartphones in classrooms. Higher ed:
  great national treasure but too expensive / too few — MOOCs (Coursera, Andrew Ng's 120k-student ML course;
  2/3 of Coursera students outside US). Root problem: students/teachers/parents not in any rational economic
  system. 
- **Healthcare = the most screwed-up part of the US economy** — $3T (would be the world's 5th-largest
  country; bigger than all of France's GDP); ~1/3 wasted (~$1T) on overutilization/errors; patients/
  providers/payers not rationally linked. The exciting moment: **Big Data + Affordable Care Act +
  entrepreneurs**. "If I were building my career now I'd major in CS and big data and work in this field."

## China, women in tech, misc (~40:15–end)
- China: very hard for Western firms; a fast-tracked law would require hosting Chinese-citizen data locally
  + a "front door" + source code → might push Apple out. Succeed only by partnering with local champions
  (LinkedIn, Evernote). Google's pull-out over censorship — "tough call."
- Women in tech: "terrible." VC even worse (~4% women) vs his own firm ~20% ("we make better decisions,
  and we're more attractive to female founders"). Causes: curriculum (solitary programming, not leveraging
  leadership), role models, lack of mentoring. "A national waste."
- Women breaking into VC: you can't BUY the right to advise founders — earn it by being a successful
  entrepreneur first. VC is a **service industry**: "we do not run these companies; we serve the
  entrepreneurs… the reason we invested is their vision is better than ours."
- Resume: substance over burnishing, but a Google internship genuinely moves you up. Social
  entrepreneurship: "entrepreneurs do more than anyone thinks possible with less than anyone thinks
  possible" — Yunus's microloans, Bono's (RED). Harder, but impact can be greater.
- Startups that met all 5 criteria but still failed: usually **too early** (Friendster, MySpace) or **didn't
  execute**. "Ideas are easy, execution is everything. It takes a team to win."
- His contact gimmick: email him your 3 favorite life-changing books and he'll send the slides/whiteboard.
