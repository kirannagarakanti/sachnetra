Chapter 2: What are small cap, micro cap, and nano-cap? 3:37 — Large cap = Shahrukh/Amitabh (everyone sees them). Small cap = Vicky Kaushal / Kartik Aaryan (growing fast). Micro cap = background artists, on the journey to becoming small cap. Nano caps = those who haven't even given an audition yet. Penny stock vs nano cap: a penny stock has no business at all (bad business); a nano cap has a good business but is not discovered yet — factory in a corner/village, but contributing meaningfully to real GDP. Entering before institutions even identify it. Institutions have one rule: liquidity (how much, how quickly can you buy/sell). If you can't build a significant position in 1–3 days, institutions won't cater to it. A 500-cr-market-cap company may trade ~10 lakh/day, so building a 2% position takes 2–3 months. Institutions only come when a company gives clear visibility 500cr → 5000cr; they're comfortable at 5000, not 500. "When it reaches 5000, we exit and they enter."

Chapter 3: How to identify nano-cap stocks? 6:26 — Everyone has information on large caps (thousands of reports, MFs, insurance, finance platforms) → no information asymmetry there. Big money (200–500%+) only happens when undiscovered information gets discovered. In large caps everything is discovered. In our companies "you have to research so much that you should know more than the company." Large caps need analyst/annual/brokerage reports; "in my case I just need conviction."

Chapter 4: How do you identify a Multibagger Stock? 7:55 — "We don't have any filter for generating any idea." Other analysts use filters (earnings/growth/profit/cash/debt). We go down to the ground: talk to promoters, verify their claims (owners only tell you good things). If a promoter claims 1000 → 5000 sales in 3 years: how is the growth financed, where's the market for the extra 4000, how do you take share, will other players sit still? We do balance-sheet analysis, talk to auditors, government agencies, stakeholders, customers, employees. "When the cycle comes you keep your entire arsenal ready and fire; when there's no cycle you keep quiet." (Host: "like an arranged-marriage private investigator.") Sleeper-class 12-hour trains to visit factories; sometimes help the government to help the company; vested interest because your fortunes are tied to the company's growth.

Chapter 5: Jessica Lal x Indri Whiskey connection 11:33 — Biggest multibagger: **Piccadily Agro**, recommended in the 2022 Muhurat report (no longer invested; speaker is a SEBI-registered analyst). Company owned by S. Siddharth Sharma, described as having served a 20-year jail sentence for the Jessica Lal murder case ("No One Killed Jessica"); father a prominent cabinet minister. Thesis: a promoter coming out of jail with a tarnished image has a *non-negotiable* drive to change his image → will strive for maximum growth. The market recognizes that change only when it's 50–100x; we track it when the name/image is at its worst and no one will touch it. Company was a sugar/ethanol maker; in 2021-22 found it was testing whiskey / single malt. Won runner-up at a Las Vegas whiskey tasting (~2020-21); next year became "world's best whiskey." Single malt = **Indri**; the red dot in the brand echoes the red bindi Jessica Lal wore (play on words). Invested before the first prize, on the India premium-whiskey consumption trend (India = biggest whiskey consumer; aspirational, royal). "If someone keeps coming 2nd/3rd, it's a matter of time before 1st." Now at ~8000–9000 cr market cap MFs will invest; "I have exited." "I don't want any MF/PMS/AIF/FII in any of my picks — when they come it comes to everyone's notice → the idea is half-discounted → it gets crowded → making money becomes very difficult." Risk/fear: every great investor is scared; 100 can go to 50 before 50 → 500; the fear is whether you can handle it mentally. Allocation: never more than ₹6–8 of ₹100 in one idea, so an 8→4 drop is a small % hit on the portfolio.

Chapter 7: Top market indicators to identify loss investments 22:16 — Indicators ignored: (1) ownership normally changes in **one** offer; needing three meant the promoter tussle would drag on and no one was focused on growth → should have exited (loss would've been ~15–20%). (2) **Believing the promoter** — owners only tell you the good side; "this believing the promoter was the biggest lesson in my life." (3) **Never fall in love with a stock** — fortunes can change overnight; he was emotionally invested (family is in pigments, which feed paint; he likes turnaround companies — turnarounds pay 400–6000%, not 30–40%). (4) A 12,000–13,000-cr-revenue acquirer that can't grow a petty 400–500-cr company (flat/minor growth each quarter) is itself a red flag, while Berger/Asian Paints were growing.

Chapter 8: How to make money by doing nothing? 26:37 — "Money is made in trading; **wealth** is created in investing." (Rakesh Jhunjhunwala: trading covers expenses, wealth comes from doing nothing.) Identify a good business, research it deeply, get the thesis right, then wait — it takes time to go ₹100 → ₹500 sales. Retail wants 3–5x in a year; won't happen. "The biggest money in the market is made by doing nothing."

Chapter 9: Difference between HNI investor and retail investor psychology 27:48 — Retail asks "how high will it go?"; Ultra-HNI asks "what is my maximum downside?" Because the HNI thinks downside-first, he puts 5–7% of his portfolio in (significant); retail asks upside-first and is happy with 10–20% even when the potential is 100–300%. HNI wants 5–10x; retail wants 30–40%. FD ~6.5% (≈5% post-tax). Real inflation higher than CPI: CPI counts milk/tomatoes/onions/grains/pulses, but education / hospital / medical / insurance inflation (nursery school fee ₹2.25 lakh/yr example) isn't captured — real inflation 8–12%, so analyst models using 3–4% go out of sync; "if you haven't understood where the next inflation is, you can't pick a multibagger." Retail doesn't care about the business/promoter/balance sheet — "needs a name / a tip," keeps an advisor but asks 1000 people. Smart HNI understands the business model, checks reality from outside people, goes into "Pollution Control Board mode," pulls GST data to see how much GST is paid, then forms a thesis → conviction lets him turn 1 lakh into 5 lakh; retail does 10,000 and can't build conviction.

Chapter 10: One advice for retail investors 33:22 — "Stop running behind the narrative." All winners came from uncomfortable areas. Don't chase yesterday's winner — that theme has played out. Next big money = 400–10000%, not 30–40%. The company gives clear indications of an upcoming move: real business operations start to change; a promoter who realizes his industry will grow cleans up (removes debt, changes business model, hires experienced/skillful people, markets more, wants a serious investor ecosystem to recognize him). These don't immediately show on the balance sheet/P&L, so retail never comes; smart money builds the position and sits, ready for nothing to happen for 3 years. Conviction comes only from research. "If someone is doing something groundbreaking, he won't disclose it to you — he keeps it for himself or his close investors. But we know how to extract it."

Chapter 11: What are pre-IPO Investments & their return potential? 36:44 — Pre-IPO = off-market transaction ~1 year before the IPO; the company raises a small fund from a close investor community that will support it later in the market. Example: a flower-pot maker (25% margin) that will get orders from new airports/malls. From pre-IPO to inside the IPO you can make 3–4x before the IPO even arrives. Only serious investors: liquidity (can't sell freely; wait until IPO, then a 6-month lock-in); the company must perform in that window; no easily available past record. 3–4x in ~2–3 years. Pre-IPO round (e.g. 40 cr two years before a 150-cr IPO) funds capacity/expansion so valuation is 3–4x by IPO. Promoters make the *majority* of their money at IPO (major dilution then); the pre-IPO dilution is to give well-wishers a chance. "You invest when the promoter is investing and exit when the promoter is exiting." Minimum ticket sometimes up to 2 cr, but they structure participation so even a 5-lakh allocation can join.
