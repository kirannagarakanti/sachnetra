## ELI12 — what is this actually saying?

The world has *too few things and too many people who want them* — that's the entire reason economics exists. Because stuff is scarce, every choice you make has a hidden cost: the *next-best thing you gave up* (called opportunity cost). When two people both have something the other needs (Ravi can edit videos, Amit has money but no time), they trade — and trade makes both better off, even if one person is technically better at everything (because his *time* is worth more elsewhere). Markets are just invisible meeting points where prices auto-adjust based on what people want and what's available — onion prices go up when crops fail, then farmers grow more onions next year, then prices fall. Adam Smith called this the "Invisible Hand." Money is just trust that everyone agrees has value — it's not really worth anything by itself. Banks aren't lockers; they take your deposit and lend it to someone else, charging interest. Inflation is a silent thief: if your money grows 10% but prices grow 12%, you got poorer. GDP measures total stuff produced in a country, but it ignores moms cooking dinner and counts pollution as growth. Your salary depends on how easy you are to replace, not how hard you work — that's why CEOs make 300× a junior employee. And humans aren't actually rational about money: poor people make short-term choices because long-term thinking is a luxury when you're stressed. That's the whole video in one paragraph.

## Glossary (new terms only)

> Only terms NOT already in [`wiki/glossary.md`](../../sachnetra%20v2/wiki/glossary.md) AND likely to recur in SachNetra research. Pruned aggressively — the video introduced ~25 econ terms; only the ones that will come back into SachNetra conversations are listed here.

- **Opportunity cost** — the value of the next-best alternative you gave up to do this thing. *(Why it matters: every research priority decision is implicitly an opportunity-cost question — "what experiment did I NOT run because I chose this one?")*
- **Comparative advantage** — the principle that two parties should each specialize in what they can do at the *lowest opportunity cost*, even if one party is absolutely better at everything. *(Why it matters: maps directly to the Lijo-vs-James lane split — James builds collectors because Lijo's time is dearer on the research side, not because James is better at engineering than Lijo would be.)*
- **Hyperbolic discounting** — humans value near-term rewards disproportionately more than future ones, in a way that mathematically can't be reconciled with rational discounting. Today's ₹5,000 beats next year's ₹50,000 in our brain, but not on a spreadsheet. *(Why it matters: shows up everywhere — own trader psychology, retail user behavior, why "park" verdicts are hard to keep parked.)*
- **Loss aversion** — losing ₹500 hurts roughly 2× as much as gaining ₹500 feels good. *(Why it matters: critical when designing kill signals — emotional override pressure scales with this asymmetry. Also the reason most retail traders refuse to cut losers.)*
